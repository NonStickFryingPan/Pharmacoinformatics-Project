The Objective

The goal of this project was to design a complete, end-to-end in silico drug discovery pipeline.

For our target, we selected Leucine-Rich Repeat Kinase 2 (LRRK2), a massive protein heavily implicated in Parkinson's Disease.

  • Why LRRK2? It is currently one of the hottest areas in neuro-pharmacology.
  • The Problem: Existing inhibitors suffer from off-target toxicities (lung/kidney accumulation) and poor Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) penetration.
  • Our Mission: Discover a novel, potent, and safe lead compound using computational algorithms without stepping foot in a wet lab.

Open Targets Validation

We didn't just guess our target. We queried the Open Targets GraphQL API directly from our R script.

LRRK2 possesses one of the highest genetic association scores for Parkinson's Disease (MONDO_0005180), firmly validating it as a primary pharmacological target.

The Algorithmic Inspiration

We didn't just want to build a basic pipeline. We looked to the State-of-the-Art.

"We applied our model... to identify eight antibacterial compounds that are structurally distant from known antibiotics... highlighting the utility of deep learning to discover structurally distinct molecules."

— Stokes et al. (Cell, 2020) "A Deep Learning Approach to Antibiotic Discovery" (The Halicin Paper)

The Takeaway: If you train an AI to find drugs that look exactly like current drugs, you only discover "me-too" drugs with the same side effects. We must design a system that explicitly hunts for Novelty.

Pipeline Architecture

Our methodology was broken down into 9 sequential computational modules, executed entirely in R.

[1] Target Selection -> Open Targets + PubMed
[2] Data Curation -> ChEMBL REST API
[3] Featurization -> rcdk Descriptors + ECFP4 Fingerprints
[4] QSAR Modeling -> Random Forest + Scaffold Split
[5] Chemical Space -> PCA + t-SNE Mapping
[6] Virtual Screen -> QSAR Potency + Novelty Reward
[7] 3D Preparation -> AlphaFold Colab / RCSB PDB
[8] Mol. Docking -> AutoDock Vina / PyRx
[9] Prioritization -> Lipinski + Veber + CNS MPO Score

Phase 1: Data Acquisition & Cleaning

Algorithms are only as good as the data they consume. We pulled raw bioactivity data from the ChEMBL database via REST API.

  • Unified Scale: Converted all IC50 values (nM, µM) into a standardized logarithmic scale (pIC50).
  • Deduplication: Merged duplicate entries by taking the median pIC50.
  • Filtration: Retained only precise (`=`) measurements to avoid inequality data.

Result: A high-quality matrix of verified LRRK2 inhibitors ready for machine learning.

Bioactivity Landscape

By standardizing measurements to the pIC50 (-log10 M) scale, we linearized the dose-response relationships. A pIC50 of 9 equals 1 nanomolar (nM) potency.

Phase 2: Featurization (Math from Chemistry)

Machine learning models cannot read pictures of molecules. We had to digitize the chemistry using the rcdk and fingerprint packages.

rcdk Descriptors

Calculated physicochemical properties (Molecular Weight, LogP, HBD/HBA, TPSA, RotB, FracCsp3) to feed the model structural statistics.

ECFP4 Fingerprints

Converted the topological shape of each molecule into a binary array representing the presence or absence of specific substructures up to radius 2.

Demystifying ECFP4

You can't feed a molecular sketch to a Random Forest. You need bits. We use Extended-Connectivity Fingerprints (ECFP) to hash topological neighborhoods.

[Step 1] Assign integer identifier to every atom based on valence/mass.[Step 2] ITERATION 1 (Radius 0) Atom[C] -> Hash(C) -> 10423[Step 3] ITERATION 2 (Radius 1) Atom [C] + Immediate Bonds -> Hash(10423 + Bonds) -> 55219[Step 4] ITERATION 3 (Radius 2 = ECFP4) Atom [C] + Neighbors' Neighbors -> Hash(55219 + N) -> 88910[Step 5] Fold billions of possible hashes into a fixed-length bit array.

If bit 412 is a '1', the AI knows a specific substructure exists in that molecule.

Phase 3: Building the "Virtual Brain"

We trained a Random Forest QSAR (Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship) model to predict pIC50 based on molecular shape.

Crucial Step: The Scaffold Split

Instead of a random train/test split, we used Tanimoto distance hierarchical clustering to group molecules by their core scaffolds. The AI was tested on chemical families it had never seen before. This prevents the model from "cheating" through memorization and proves true predictive power.

What Does the Model Value?

We didn't just blindly trust the AI. We interrogated it. The Random Forest %IncMSE reveals exactly which physicochemical properties drive LRRK2 inhibition.

Visualizing Chemical Space

How do you plot high-dimensional fingerprints on a 2D screen? We mapped them using PCA and t-SNE via Rtsne.

  • Unlike PCA, t-SNE preserves "local neighborhoods" of data.
  • It groups structurally similar compounds into visual "islands."
  • By coloring these islands by pIC50, we visually validated our Structure-Activity Relationships (SAR): highly potent compounds clustered together naturally.

The Pivot: Similarity vs. Novelty

This is where we applied the logic from the MIT Halicin paper.

Initially, we used the Tanimoto score to find compounds similar to known drugs. We realized this was a mistake. Known LRRK2 inhibitors are too greasy and toxic.

Novelty_Norm = 1.0 - Tanimoto_Similarity
Combined_Score = 0.5 * Novelty_Norm + 0.5 * QSAR_Predicted_pIC50

We explicitly reprogrammed our algorithm to reward Structural Novelty. We demanded the model find compounds that it predicted to be highly potent, but that looked completely different from existing therapies.

Phase 4: Physical Validation (Docking)

Our R script gave us 8 novel candidates on paper. Now we had to prove they physically fit into the protein target.

  • The Lock (AlphaFold): LRRK2 is too massive (>2500 amino acids) to process normally. We isolated the exact FASTA sequence for the Kinase Domain and folded a custom 3D .pdb receptor using the AlphaFold Colab.
  • The Key (AutoDock Vina): We simulated the physical binding of our 8 novel ligands into the AlphaFold receptor to generate a Binding Affinity score (kcal/mol).

Phase 5: Safety First (ADMET)

A molecule that binds well is useless if it kills the patient or can't reach the target.

Basic Compliance

Applied strict Lipinski's Rule of 5 and Veber filters (RotB <= 10, TPSA <= 140) to ensure oral bioavailability and general drug-likeness.

The CNS MPO Score

Parkinson's is a brain disease. We applied criteria inspired by the Pfizer Multi-Parameter Optimization algorithm to guarantee our candidates could cross the Blood-Brain Barrier.

The CNS Drug Envelope

If the drug can't cross the Blood-Brain Barrier, it is completely ineffective. Here is how our Lead Candidate maps against ideal CNS MPO boundaries.

The Final Calculation

We combined all computational evidence into a single, elegant mathematical formula in R to rank our final lead candidates.

Priority_Score = (0.20 * QSAR_Potency) +
(0.25 * Vina_Docking_Affinity) +
(0.15 * Structural_Novelty) +
(0.25 * ADMET_Safety) +
(0.15 * CNS_Bonus)

The compound scoring highest represents the ultimate calculated balance of potency, novelty, and safety.
